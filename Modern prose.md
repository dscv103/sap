# Modern Prose Style

The governing framework for all personal and business writing. Four sources form the
authority stack — **Pinker leads**; when sources conflict, Pinker wins.

| Authority | Primary Contribution | Weight |
|-----------|---------------------|--------|
| Pinker, *The Sense of Style* (2014) | Overall approach; modern usage; coherence | **Primary** |
| Zinsser, *On Writing Well* (1976/2006) | Humanity; clutter removal; nonfiction clarity | High |
| Williams & Bizup, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* | Sentence-level precision; information flow | High |
| Cutts, *The Oxford Guide to Plain English* | Plain English floor; vocabulary; document structure | Supporting |

---

## Two Modes

### Mode 1 — Drafting (generate new content)

Apply the principles below from the first word. Do not draft freely then revise — write
right the first time.

**Pre-draft checklist (apply silently):**
- [ ] Who is the reader? What do they already know? What do they need?
- [ ] What is the single most important thing to communicate? Put it early.
- [ ] Prefer short sentences by default. Vary length for rhythm.
- [ ] Choose characters as grammatical subjects; actions as verbs.
- [ ] Write in first person where appropriate — a human voice beats a corporate voice.
- [ ] Match register to document type (see Document Types section).

---

### Mode 2 — Revision / Rewrite (edit existing text)

When the user provides existing text to improve:

#### Step 1 — Brief Critique

Deliver a 3–6 sentence summary that:
- Names the main problems as patterns, not every individual instance
- Notes what is already working
- Frames the approach for the rewrite

Keep it direct. Do not soften problems into vague encouragement.

#### Step 2 — Revised Draft with Inline Citations

Produce the rewritten text. After each meaningfully changed passage, add a short
parenthetical source citation:

> The committee approved the proposal. *(Pinker: characters as subjects; active verb)*

Citations are brief labels — not explanations. A single summary citation at the end
of a restructured section is sufficient:
> *(Zinsser: clutter removed; W&B: nominalizations restored to verbs)*

## Core Principles

### 1. Classic Style — Show, Don't Report *(Pinker)*

Imagine you are standing beside the reader, pointing at the world through a clear
window. Your job is to show what you see, not to perform expertise or demonstrate effort.

- Write as if the reader is intelligent but uninformed on this specific topic.
- Avoid metadiscourse: do not narrate your own writing.
  - Cut: *"In this email, I will address three concerns."* → Begin with the first concern.
- Minimize hedging. Qualify only when the qualification is genuinely meaningful.
- Avoid the curse of knowledge: what is obvious to you is not obvious to your reader.
  Supply the context they need, then get out of the way.

---

### 2. Strip the Clutter *(Zinsser + Cutts)*

Every word that does not serve the reader must go.

**Common clutter to cut on sight:**

| Pattern | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Throat-clearing opener | "I wanted to reach out because..." | Start with the point |
| Empty closer | "Feel free to reach out with any questions." | Cut or make it specific |
| Redundant pair | "each and every", "first and foremost" | Pick one word |
| Latinate inflation | "utilize", "endeavor", "commence" | "use", "try", "start" |
| Vague intensifier | "very", "quite", "rather", "somewhat" | Remove or find a stronger word |
| Hedge stack | "may perhaps be somewhat..." | State the position |
| Filler phrase | "it is the case that", "the fact that" | Delete entirely |

---

### 3. Characters and Actions *(Williams & Bizup)*

Readers process prose most easily when **characters are grammatical subjects** and
**actions are verbs**. This single principle fixes most awkward business prose.

- Weak: *"An evaluation of the vendor proposals was completed by procurement."*
- Strong: *"Procurement evaluated the vendor proposals."*

**Nominalization** — turning verbs into nouns — is the primary enemy:

| Nominalization | Restore to verb |
|----------------|-----------------|
| make a decision | decide |
| conduct an analysis | analyze |
| provide assistance | help |
| have a discussion | discuss |
| reach an agreement | agree |
| give consideration to | consider |
| the implementation of | implementing |

---

### 4. Information Flow *(Williams & Bizup + Pinker)*

Readers build understanding by linking each new sentence to what came before.

- **Open each sentence with familiar context** — something just mentioned or assumed
  known by the reader.
- **End each sentence with the new, important information** — this is where natural
  emphasis falls.
- **Topic sentences state the paragraph's claim.** The rest of the paragraph provides
  evidence, elaboration, or qualification.

Poor flow:
> *"There are three main risks. The first risk is timeline slippage, which procurement
> flagged. Budget overruns were the second concern raised. Vendor reliability was also
> mentioned."*

Improved flow:
> *"Procurement flagged three risks: timeline slippage, budget overruns, and vendor
> reliability. Of these, timeline slippage is most likely to affect launch."*

---

### 5. Sentence Length and Rhythm *(Cutts + Pinker)*

- Target average: **15–20 words** per sentence in business prose.
- Short sentences punch. Use them after long ones.
- Never allow three consecutive long sentences — break the pattern.
- Read the draft aloud. If you run out of breath, the sentence is too long.
- Vary structure: not every sentence should follow subject → verb → object.

---

### 6. Plain English Vocabulary *(Cutts)*

Prefer the short, common English word over the long Latinate one — unless the longer
word is genuinely more precise.

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help, enable |
| leverage (as a verb) | use, apply |
| communicate | say, tell, write |
| in the event that | if |
| with regard to | about |
| prior to | before |
| subsequent to | after |
| in order to | to |
| at this point in time | now |
| on a going-forward basis | from now on |
| it is important to note that | (delete; just note it) |

---

### 7. Modern Usage — Ignore the Fake Rules *(Pinker)*

These "rules" are prescriptive myths. Following them produces stiff, unnatural prose.

- ✓ Start a sentence with *And*, *But*, or *Because* — it is often the clearest connector.
- ✓ Split infinitives when it reads more naturally (*to clearly state*, not *to state clearly*
  when the adverb modifies the infinitive specifically).
- ✓ End a sentence with a preposition (*the colleague I spoke to* is fine).
- ✓ Use singular *they* for an unspecified person.
- ✗ Avoid *very unique*, *most essential*, *less perfect* — logical absolutes are not gradable.
- ✗ Do not use passive voice to hide who is responsible for something; use it only when
  the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or the object is the natural topic of the sentence.

## Tone Calibration

Clarity and directness are the defaults — but the appropriate register depends on
the situation. Assess two variables before writing or rewriting:

**Stakes:** How significant is the outcome for the reader?
**Relationship:** How much goodwill is in the account between writer and reader?

Use both to place the document on this scale:

| Situation | Stakes | Relationship | Register |
|-----------|--------|--------------|----------|
| Routine update, internal | Low | Established | Direct, brief, warm |
| Request or ask | Moderate | Established | Direct, polite, specific |
| Bad news (process, policy) | Moderate | Neutral | Matter-of-fact; acknowledge impact |
| Bad news (personal impact) | High | Any | Lead with acknowledgment; be human first |
| Complaint or dispute | Moderate–High | Strained | Firm, factual, non-accusatory |
| Disciplinary or corrective | High | Any | Private, specific, non-punitive in tone |
| Relationship repair | High | Damaged | Acknowledge first; do not lead with facts |

### Tone Techniques by Situation

**Routine / informational:**
No special softening needed. Lead with the point. Be warm but efficient.

**Request or ask:**
State the ask directly in sentence one, then give the reason. Readers prefer knowing
what is wanted before being told why.
> *"Could you review the attached draft by Thursday? We want your input before the
> client call on Friday."*
Not: *"Given that the client call is on Friday and we want to incorporate feedback
beforehand, we were wondering if you might be available to review the draft..."*

**Bad news — process or policy:**
Lead with the decision. Do not hide it in paragraph three. Acknowledge the impact
briefly, then explain. Burying bad news reads as evasive and damages trust more than
the news itself.
> *"We are not moving forward with your proposal. [One sentence reason.] We
> appreciated the work you put into it and welcome you to submit again next cycle."*

**Bad news — personal impact (layoffs, rejections, serious discipline):**
Lead with acknowledgment before the decision. This is the one case where you do not
open with the main point — the reader needs to feel heard before they can process
information.
> *"I know this is difficult news. [Decision, plainly stated.] [What happens next,
> concretely.]"*
Keep it short. Do not over-explain or over-apologize — both read as self-serving.

**Complaint or dispute (to a vendor, institution, or counterparty):**
State the facts without attributing motive. Name the problem, the impact, and what
you want — in that order. Accusatory language weakens your position; let the facts do
the work.
> *"Invoice #4872 charges $14,200. Our purchase order #PO-311 authorized $13,450.
> Please send a corrected invoice by [date] so we can release payment."*

**Disciplinary or corrective (internal):**
Be specific, not general. Name the behavior, not the character. State the expectation
going forward. Avoid language that sounds performative or HR-scripted — it distances
the reader and signals the writer is covering themselves rather than trying to fix
something.
- Weak: *"It has come to our attention that there have been concerns raised..."*
- Strong: *"Last Tuesday's report was submitted two days late without notice. Going
  forward, flag delays the day before the deadline so we can adjust."*

**Relationship repair:**
Acknowledge before explaining. An explanation that leads — especially one that sounds
like a defense — makes things worse. The structure is: acknowledge → take
responsibility → state what changes → close warmly.
Do not over-apologize (it becomes about you) and do not end with a question that puts
the burden back on the reader.

### What Tone Calibration Does Not Change

Tone adjusts warmth, ordering, and register — it does not license clutter, padding,
or vagueness. A sensitive email still has no throat-clearing openers. A firm
complaint letter still uses plain English. The core principles always apply; tone
calibration only changes *how* they are applied.

## Document Types

### Email

- **Subject line:** specific enough to act on.
  - Weak: *"Update"*
  - Strong: *"Q2 Budget Approved — Action Needed by Friday"*
- Lead with the most important sentence. No warm-up.
- One primary ask per email. If multiple, number them.
- Closing: concrete action + deadline, or nothing.
- Length: three paragraphs for most emails; five is the absolute ceiling.

### Letter / Formal Correspondence

- Opening paragraph: state the purpose in one sentence.
- Body: one topic per paragraph; paragraphs rarely exceed five sentences.
- Closing: state the next step or desired outcome clearly.
- Tone: formal but human — not cold, not chatty.

### Memo / Internal Report

- Lead with the conclusion (inverted pyramid). The reader knows your point after
  paragraph one.
- Use headers for anything over one page; make them descriptive, not clever.
- Bullet points are appropriate for three or more parallel items — not as a substitute
  for paragraphs with a logical argument.
- Every paragraph must earn its place. Cut anything that repeats.

### Paper / Essay

- Thesis in the first paragraph — do not make the reader hunt for it.
- Each body paragraph: **issue sentence** (the claim) → evidence → link back to thesis.
- Transitions carry meaning; they are not just sequence signals.
  - Weak: *"Next, I will discuss..."* → cut the metadiscourse; make the transition
    logical.
- Conclusion: do not summarize what was just said. Synthesize; tell the reader what
  it means or what follows from it.

## Common Violations Quick Reference

**Throat-clearing opener:**
> *"I am writing to inform you that the meeting has been rescheduled."*
→ *"The Monday meeting has been rescheduled to Wednesday, April 16 at 2 p.m."*

**Nominalization chain:**
> *"The implementation of the new system requires the completion of additional training."*
→ *"Implementing the new system requires additional training."*

**Passive with no agent:**
> *"It has been decided that the project will be delayed."*
→ *"We are delaying the project."* (or name who decided)

**Buried main point:**
> Three paragraphs of context before the ask.
→ State the ask in sentence one. Context follows.

**Hedge stacking:**
> *"It may perhaps be somewhat premature to make a final determination..."*
→ *"It is too early to decide."*

**Empty closer:**
> *"Please feel free to reach out if you have any questions or concerns."*
→ Cut it, or make it specific: *"Call me at [number] if the deadline is a problem."*

**Metadiscourse opener:**
> *"In this memo, I will outline three reasons why the proposal should be rejected."*
→ *"The proposal should be rejected for three reasons."*

## Citation Format (Revision Mode)

Short parenthetical labels after each changed passage — not footnotes, not long
explanations:

- *(Pinker: classic style — show, not report)*
- *(Pinker: fake rule — preposition at end is fine)*
- *(Zinsser: clutter — removed filler phrase)*
- *(Zinsser: throat-clearing opener — cut)*
- *(W&B: nominalization restored to verb)*
- *(W&B: information flow — new information to end of sentence)*
- *(W&B: characters as subjects)*
- *(Cutts: plain English — shorter word)*
- *(Cutts: sentence too long — split)*
- *(All: active voice)*
